# Introduction

Electrical power systems are designed to deliver electrical energy at a constant voltage, constant frequency, and with a sinusoidal waveform. In an ideal power system, consumers receive uninterrupted power at the rated voltage and frequency without any distortion.

However, in practical power systems, various disturbances such as voltage fluctuations, harmonics, switching operations, lightning, and faults affect the quality of the electrical supply. These disturbances may cause malfunction, overheating, reduced efficiency, or even failure of electrical and electronic equipment.

The term **Power Quality** refers to the ability of the power system to supply electrical energy with acceptable voltage, frequency, and waveform characteristics so that electrical equipment operates properly and efficiently.

With the increasing use of computers, microprocessors, variable speed drives, renewable energy systems, and other sensitive electronic equipment, maintaining good power quality has become one of the major challenges in modern power systems.

---

# What is Power Quality?

Power quality is defined as the ability of an electrical power system to supply electrical energy at the specified voltage, frequency, and waveform without interruptions or excessive disturbances.

A good quality power supply should have:

- Constant voltage magnitude.
- Constant frequency.
- Pure sinusoidal waveform.
- Minimum harmonics.
- No interruptions.
- No excessive voltage fluctuations.
- Reliable and continuous power supply.

If any of these characteristics deviate beyond acceptable limits, the power quality deteriorates.

---

# Ideal Power Supply

An ideal electrical power supply should possess the following characteristics:

- Rated voltage.
- Rated frequency (50 Hz or 60 Hz).
- Sinusoidal voltage waveform.
- Balanced three-phase voltages.
- Continuous and uninterrupted supply.
- Minimum electrical noise.
- No voltage sags or swells.
- Low harmonic distortion.

# Voltage Sag (Voltage Dip)

Voltage sag, also known as **voltage dip**, is a short-duration reduction in the RMS value of the supply voltage.

According to IEEE standards, a voltage sag occurs when the supply voltage decreases to **10%–90% of its rated value** for a duration ranging from **0.5 cycle to 1 minute**.

Voltage sag is one of the most common power quality problems encountered in industrial power systems.

---

## Causes of Voltage Sag

Voltage sag may occur due to:

- Short-circuit faults.
- Starting of large induction motors.
- Sudden increase in load.
- Transformer energization.
- Faults in nearby feeders.
- Utility system disturbances.

---

## Effects of Voltage Sag

Voltage sag can cause:

- Motor speed reduction.
- Relay malfunction.
- Computer shutdown.
- PLC reset.
- Process interruption.
- Lighting dimness.
- Production loss in industries.

---

## Methods to Reduce Voltage Sag

Voltage sag can be minimized by:

- Using Dynamic Voltage Restorers (DVR).
- Installing UPS systems.
- Proper feeder design.
- Using separate feeders for sensitive loads.
- Fast fault clearing.
- Proper voltage regulation.

---

# Voltage Swell

Voltage swell is a short-duration increase in the RMS value of the supply voltage.

According to IEEE standards, voltage swell occurs when the voltage rises to **110%–180% of the rated value** for a duration of **0.5 cycle to 1 minute**.

Although less common than voltage sag, voltage swell can also damage electrical equipment.

---

## Causes of Voltage Swell

Common causes include:

- Sudden removal of heavy loads.
- Single line-to-ground faults.
- Capacitor bank switching.
- Incorrect transformer tap settings.
- Poor voltage regulation.

---

## Effects of Voltage Swell

Voltage swell may result in:

- Insulation stress.
- Overheating of equipment.
- Failure of electronic devices.
- Lamp damage.
- Reduced equipment life.
- Protective relay operation.

---

## Methods to Reduce Voltage Swell

Voltage swell can be controlled by:

- Automatic Voltage Regulators (AVR).
- Proper transformer tap control.
- Surge protection devices.
- Voltage regulation equipment.
- Proper system design.

---

# Harmonics

Harmonics are voltage or current components whose frequencies are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.

For a power system operating at **50 Hz**, the harmonic frequencies are:

- 2nd Harmonic = 100 Hz
- 3rd Harmonic = 150 Hz
- 5th Harmonic = 250 Hz
- 7th Harmonic = 350 Hz

The presence of harmonics distorts the ideal sinusoidal waveform and degrades power quality.

---

# Fundamental Frequency

The normal operating frequency of an AC power system is called the **fundamental frequency**.

For most countries,

- 50 Hz (India, Europe)
- 60 Hz (USA, Canada)

An ideal waveform contains only the fundamental frequency.

---

# Harmonic Order

The harmonic order is the ratio of the harmonic frequency to the fundamental frequency.

Mathematically,

\[
n=\frac{f_h}{f}
\]

where

- \(n\) = Harmonic order
- \(f_h\) = Harmonic frequency
- \(f\) = Fundamental frequency

Example:

For a 250 Hz component in a 50 Hz system,

\[
n=\frac{250}{50}=5
\]

Hence, it is called the **5th harmonic**.

---

# Sources of Harmonics

Harmonics are mainly produced by **nonlinear loads**.

Common sources include:

- Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs)
- Rectifiers
- Inverters
- UPS systems
- Switched Mode Power Supplies (SMPS)
- LED lighting
- Battery chargers
- Arc furnaces
- Computers
- Television sets

---

# Effects of Harmonics

Harmonics have several undesirable effects on power systems.

They may cause:

- Overheating of transformers.
- Overheating of motors.
- Increased transmission losses.
- Reduced power factor.
- False operation of protective relays.
- Capacitor bank failures.
- Excessive neutral current.
- Equipment malfunction.
- Reduced equipment life.
- Communication interference.

---

# Total Harmonic Distortion (THD)

The amount of waveform distortion is measured using **Total Harmonic Distortion (THD)**.

Mathematically,

\[
THD=
\frac{\sqrt{V_2^2+V_3^2+V_4^2+\cdots}}
{V_1}
\times100\%
\]

where

- \(V_1\) = Fundamental voltage
- \(V_2,V_3,\ldots\) = Harmonic voltages

Lower THD indicates better power quality.

---

- Active power filters.
- Line reactors.
- Isolation transformers.
- Harmonic filters.
- Multi-pulse converters.
- Proper system design.

# Transients

A **transient** is a sudden, short-duration change in voltage or current that occurs in a power system.

Transients last for a very short period, typically from a few **microseconds** to a few **milliseconds**, but they may have very high magnitude.

Although their duration is short, transients can seriously damage electrical and electronic equipment.

---

# Types of Transients

Power system transients are broadly classified into:

- Impulsive Transients
- Oscillatory Transients

---

## 1. Impulsive Transients

Impulsive transients are sudden, non-power-frequency changes in voltage or current having a single polarity.

These are usually high-amplitude voltage spikes.

### Causes

- Lightning strikes.
- Electrostatic discharge.
- Switching of inductive loads.
- Fault clearing operations.

### Effects

- Insulation failure.
- Damage to electronic equipment.
- Failure of semiconductor devices.
- Data loss.

---

## 2. Oscillatory Transients

Oscillatory transients consist of voltage or current oscillations that gradually decrease with time.

These oscillations occur due to the interaction of system inductance and capacitance.

### Causes

- Capacitor bank switching.
- Transformer energization.
- Circuit breaker operation.
- Switching of transmission lines.

### Effects

- Equipment stress.
- Protective relay malfunction.
- Increased insulation stress.
- Power quality deterioration.

---

# Methods to Reduce Transients

Transients can be minimized by:

- Surge arresters.
- Surge suppressors.
- Proper grounding.
- Shielded cables.
- RC snubber circuits.
- Controlled switching devices.

---

# Voltage Imbalance

Voltage imbalance occurs when the magnitudes or phase angles of the three-phase voltages are not equal.

A balanced three-phase system should have equal voltages separated by **120°**.

Any deviation results in voltage imbalance.

---

# Causes of Voltage Imbalance

- Unequal single-phase loading.
- Blown fuse in one phase.
- Faulty transformer connections.
- Unbalanced distribution systems.
- Open conductor.

---

# Effects of Voltage Imbalance

Voltage imbalance may cause:

- Motor overheating.
- Reduced motor efficiency.
- Increased losses.
- Excessive vibration.
- Reduced equipment life.
- Nuisance tripping.

---

# Methods to Reduce Voltage Imbalance

- Balance single-phase loads.
- Maintain equal phase loading.
- Repair faulty connections.
- Proper transformer maintenance.
- Periodic system inspection.

---

# Power Quality Improvement Techniques

Power quality improvement involves the use of various devices and techniques to reduce electrical disturbances and ensure a reliable, efficient, and uninterrupted power supply.

The selection of a suitable improvement technique depends on the type of power quality problem.

---

# Harmonic Filters

Harmonic filters are devices used to reduce or eliminate harmonic currents and voltages from the power system.

They improve the waveform and reduce Total Harmonic Distortion (THD).

---

## Types of Harmonic Filters

### 1. Passive Filters

Passive filters consist of:

- Inductors
- Capacitors
- Resistors

They are designed to absorb specific harmonic frequencies.

#### Advantages

- Simple construction.
- Low cost.
- Reliable operation.
- Easy maintenance.

#### Limitations

- Fixed compensation.
- May cause resonance.
- Less effective under varying load conditions.

---

### 2. Active Power Filters (APF)

Active power filters use power electronic converters to inject compensating currents into the system.

They can eliminate multiple harmonics simultaneously.

#### Advantages

- Dynamic operation.
- High harmonic compensation.
- No resonance problems.
- Suitable for varying loads.

#### Limitations

- High initial cost.
- More complex control system.

---

# Dynamic Voltage Restorer (DVR)

A Dynamic Voltage Restorer (DVR) is a power electronic device used to protect sensitive loads from voltage sag and voltage swell.

It injects the required voltage in series with the supply to maintain the desired load voltage.

---

## Applications of DVR

- Industrial plants.
- Data centres.
- Hospitals.
- Semiconductor industries.
- Commercial buildings.

---

# Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS)

A UPS provides continuous electrical power to critical loads during supply interruptions.

It uses batteries or other energy storage devices to maintain the power supply until the main source is restored.

---

## Applications of UPS

- Computers.
- Servers.
- Hospitals.
- Communication systems.
- Banking systems.
- Control rooms.

---

# Surge Protection Devices (SPD)

Surge Protection Devices protect electrical equipment from high-voltage transients caused by lightning or switching operations.

They divert excessive surge energy safely to the ground.

---

## Applications

- Residential buildings.
- Industrial installations.
- Telecommunication equipment.
- Distribution panels.

---

# Static VAR Compensator (SVC)

A Static VAR Compensator (SVC) is a FACTS device used to provide fast reactive power compensation.

It helps maintain constant voltage and reduces voltage fluctuations.

---

## Advantages

- Improves voltage regulation.
- Reduces voltage flicker.
- Improves power factor.
- Enhances system stability.

---

# STATCOM

A Static Synchronous Compensator (STATCOM) is an advanced FACTS device that provides rapid reactive power support.

Compared with an SVC, a STATCOM offers faster response and better performance, especially during low-voltage conditions.

---

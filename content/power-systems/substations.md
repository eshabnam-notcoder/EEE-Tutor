# 1. Introduction to Substations

A substation is an important part of an electrical power system where electrical energy is transformed, controlled, protected, and distributed.

Substations act as intermediate points between:

- Generating stations
- Transmission networks
- Distribution systems
- Consumers

They ensure that electrical power is delivered safely and efficiently at the required voltage level.

---

# Definition

A **substation** is a part of an electrical power system where voltage transformation, switching, protection, and control of electrical power are carried out.

---

# Need for Substations

Electrical power is generated at relatively low voltages and transmitted over long distances at very high voltages.

Substations are required for:

- Voltage transformation
- Power transmission
- Power distribution
- System protection
- Switching operations
- Voltage regulation
- Fault isolation

# 6. Busbar Arrangements in Substations

A **busbar** is a common electrical conductor used to collect and distribute power within a substation.

All incoming and outgoing circuits are connected to busbars through switching devices such as:

- Circuit breakers
- Isolators

The arrangement of busbars determines:

- Reliability
- Flexibility
- Cost
- Maintenance capability

---

# Functions of Busbars

Busbars perform the following functions:

- Collect power from incoming lines
- Distribute power to outgoing feeders
- Provide connection points for equipment
- Allow switching flexibility

---

# Basic Busbar Arrangement

```
Incoming Lines

     │
     │
     ▼

   Busbar

     │
 ┌───┴───┐

 ▼       ▼

Transformer  Feeder

```

---

# Factors Affecting Busbar Selection

The choice of busbar arrangement depends on:

- Required reliability
- Substation voltage level
- Cost limitations
- Number of incoming/outgoing feeders
- Maintenance requirements
- Future expansion

---

# Types of Busbar Arrangements

Common busbar schemes are:

1. Single busbar system
2. Single busbar with sectionalizer
3. Main and transfer bus system
4. Double busbar system
5. Ring bus system
6. Breaker-and-a-half scheme

---

# 7. Single Busbar System

The simplest and most economical busbar arrangement.

All circuits are connected to one common busbar.

---

# Single Busbar Arrangement

```
        Incoming

           │

          CB

           │

================================
            BUS
================================

     │             │

     ▼             ▼

Transformer     Feeder

```

---

# Operation

During normal operation:

- All circuits are connected to the same busbar.
- Power flows through the busbar to outgoing feeders.

---

# Advantages of Single Busbar System

- Simple design
- Low installation cost
- Easy operation
- Requires minimum equipment

---

# Disadvantages

- Low reliability
- Complete shutdown during busbar fault
- Maintenance requires interruption of supply
- Not suitable for important substations

---

# Applications

Used in:

- Small substations
- Distribution substations
- Low and medium voltage systems

---

# 8. Single Busbar with Sectionalizer

In this arrangement, the busbar is divided into sections using a bus coupler.

---

# Arrangement

```
        Section A        Section B

==================================
             BUS

              │

          Bus Coupler

              │

==================================

```

---

# Working

During normal operation:

- Both sections operate together.

During fault:

- Faulty section can be isolated.
- Healthy section continues operation.

---

# Advantages

- Improved reliability compared with single busbar
- Allows partial maintenance
- Reduces outage area

---

# Disadvantages

- Higher cost
- Additional circuit breaker required

This arrangement uses:

- Main busbar

---

================================

        │       │

        CB     CB

             Main Bus

================================

```

---

# Operation

During normal operation:

- Circuits are connected to the main bus.

During maintenance:

- Load is transferred to the transfer bus.

---

# Advantages

- Improved maintenance flexibility
- Supply continuity during breaker maintenance

---

# Disadvantages

- More equipment required
- Higher cost
- More complex operation

# 11. Ring Bus Arrangement

In a ring bus system, all circuit breakers are connected in a closed loop.

Each circuit is connected between two circuit breakers.

---

# Ring Bus Diagram

```
          CB1
       ┌───────┐
       │       │
       │       │
      CB4     CB2
       │       │
       │       │
       └───────┘
          CB3

```

---

# Working

If one section fails:

- The faulty section is isolated.
- Remaining sections continue operation.

---

# Advantages

- High reliability
- Fault isolation capability
- Requires fewer breakers than double bus systems
- Suitable for medium and high voltage systems

---

# Disadvantages

- Expansion is difficult
- Protection becomes complex for large systems

---

# Applications

Used in:

- Transmission substations
- Industrial power systems

---

# 12. Breaker-and-a-Half Scheme

The breaker-and-a-half scheme is a highly reliable busbar arrangement used in large substations.

Each two circuits share three circuit breakers.

---

# Breaker-and-a-Half Arrangement

```
Bus 1
================================

      CB       CB       CB

       │        │        │

      Line    Line    Line


================================
Bus 2

```

---

# Meaning of "Breaker-and-a-Half"

For every two circuits:

- Three circuit breakers are used.
- Each circuit has access to one full breaker.
- The middle breaker is shared.

Hence:

\[
\frac{3\ breakers}{2\ circuits}
\]

= 1.5 breakers per circuit

---

# Operation

If one breaker fails:

- The remaining breakers maintain supply.

If one bus fails:

- Circuits can continue through the other bus.

---

# Advantages

- Very high reliability
- Suitable for EHV systems
- Allows maintenance without interruption
- Good fault tolerance

---

# Disadvantages

- High cost
- Complex protection
- Requires skilled operation

---

# Applications

Used in:

- 400 kV substations
- 765 kV substations
- Major grid substations

---

# 13. Comparison of Busbar Arrangements

| Arrangement | Reliability | Cost | Application |
|-------------|-------------|------|-------------|
| Single Bus | Low | Low | Small substations |
| Sectionalized Bus | Medium | Moderate | Distribution systems |
| Main and Transfer Bus | Medium-High | Moderate | Transmission substations |
| Double Bus | High | High | Important substations |
| Ring Bus | High | Moderate | HV systems |
| Breaker-and-a-Half | Very High | Very High | EHV substations |

# 14. Selection of Busbar Scheme

The selection depends on:

## Reliability Requirement

Critical substations require:

- Ring bus
- Double bus
- Breaker-and-a-half schemes

---

## Cost Consideration

Small substations prefer:

- Single bus arrangement

Large substations require:

- Higher reliability schemes

---

## Maintenance Requirement

If continuous operation is required:

- Double bus
- Breaker-and-a-half

are preferred.

---

# Key Points

- Busbar arrangement determines substation reliability and flexibility.
- Single busbar is simple and economical but has low reliability.
- Double busbar provides better operational flexibility.
- Ring bus provides fault isolation capability.
- Breaker-and-a-half scheme is used for highly important EHV substations.
- Higher reliability arrangements require more equipment and higher cost.
- Selection of busbar scheme depends on voltage level, reliability, and economic factors.

# 16. Power Transformer

A power transformer is the most important equipment in a substation.

It transfers electrical power between different voltage levels using electromagnetic induction.

---

# Transformer Principle

A transformer works based on:

\[
E = 4.44 f N \Phi_m
\]

where:

- \(E\) = induced EMF
- \(f\) = frequency
- \(N\) = number of turns
- \(\Phi_m\) = maximum flux

---

# Function of Power Transformer

Power transformers perform:

- Step-up voltage transformation
- Step-down voltage transformation
- Electrical isolation
- Power transfer between networks

---

# Transformer in Power System

```
Generation

   11 kV

      │

      ▼

Step-Up Transformer

      │

    220 kV

      │

      ▼

Transmission

      │

      ▼

Step-Down Transformer

      │

     11 kV

      │

      ▼

Distribution
```

---

# Types of Power Transformers

## 1. Step-Up Transformer

Used at generating stations.

Function:

\[
Low\ Voltage \rightarrow High\ Voltage
\]

Example:

\[
11kV \rightarrow 220kV
\]

---

## 2. Step-Down Transformer

Used in transmission and distribution substations.

Function:

\[
High\ Voltage \rightarrow Low\ Voltage
\]

Example:

\[
220kV \rightarrow 11kV
\]

# 19. Current Transformer (CT)

A Current Transformer is an instrument transformer used to reduce high currents to measurable values.

---

# Need for CT

Power system currents can be thousands of amperes.

Direct measurement is impossible.

CT provides:

- Safe measurement
- Protection input
- Metering input

---

# CT Working Principle

CT works on transformer action.

\[
\frac{I_p}{I_s}=\frac{N_s}{N_p}
\]

where:

- \(I_p\) = Primary current
- \(I_s\) = Secondary current
- \(N_p\) = Primary turns
- \(N_s\) = Secondary turns

---

# CT Applications

Used for:

- Overcurrent protection
- Differential protection
- Energy measurement
- Metering

---

# CT Safety Rule

The secondary of a CT should never be open-circuited while energized.

Reason:

- Very high voltage can develop
- Insulation damage may occur

---

# 20. Potential Transformer (PT)

A Potential Transformer reduces high voltage to a low measurable voltage.

It is also called:

- Voltage Transformer (VT)

---

# Function of PT

Provides voltage signals for:

- Metering
- Protection
- Control systems

---

# PT Voltage Relationship

\[
\frac{V_p}{V_s}=\frac{N_p}{N_s}
\]

where:

- \(V_p\) = Primary voltage
- \(V_s\) = Secondary voltage

---

# Capacitive Voltage Transformer (CVT)

For very high voltages, CVTs are used.

Advantages:

- Economical for EHV systems
- Provides communication coupling for PLCC

---

# CT vs PT

| CT | PT |
|----|----|
| Measures current | Measures voltage |
| Connected in series | Connected in parallel |
| Reduces current | Reduces voltage |
| Secondary should not be open | Secondary should not be shorted |

---

A lightning arrester protects substation equipment from:

- Lightning surges

- Low resistance path

      ▼

      │

      ▼

      │

      ▼

Equipment Protected
```

---

# Types of Lightning Arresters

- Rod gap arrester
- Horn gap arrester
- Metal oxide arrester (MOA)

---

# Metal Oxide Arrester

Modern substations use:

- Zinc oxide (ZnO) arresters

Advantages:

- Fast response
- Better protection
- High reliability

---

# Key Points

- Power transformers are the main voltage conversion equipment in substations.
- Circuit breakers interrupt fault currents and protect the system.
- Isolators provide safe isolation during maintenance.
- CTs and PTs allow safe measurement of high currents and voltages.
- SF₆ and vacuum circuit breakers are widely used in modern substations.
- Lightning arresters protect equipment from surge voltages.

# 22. Air Insulated Substation (AIS)

An Air Insulated Substation (AIS) is a substation where air is used as the primary insulating medium between electrical equipment.

In AIS:

- Busbars
- Circuit breakers
- Isolators
- Transformers

are installed in open air with sufficient spacing for insulation.

---

# Principle of AIS

Air acts as the dielectric medium between conductors and earth.

The insulation distance depends on:

- System voltage
- Atmospheric conditions
- Safety requirements

---

# AIS Layout

```

      │

      ▼

   Isolator

      │

      ▼

 Circuit Breaker

      │

      ▼

   Busbar

      │

      ▼

 Transformer

      │

      ▼

 Outgoing Feeder
```

---

# Advantages of AIS

- Simple construction
- Lower installation cost
- Easy inspection
- Easy maintenance
- Fault location is easier

---

# Disadvantages of AIS

- Requires large land area
- Exposed to environmental conditions
- More affected by pollution
- Higher maintenance requirements

---

# Applications of AIS

AIS is commonly used in:

- Rural substations
- High voltage transmission substations
- Large open areas

# 23. Gas Insulated Substation (GIS)

A Gas Insulated Substation uses a gas as the insulating medium instead of air.

The equipment is enclosed inside grounded metallic chambers filled with insulating gas.

The commonly used gas is:

# GIS Construction

- Metal enclosure
- Gas insulation

Protection is one of the most important functions of a substation.

The main objective of protection is to:

- Detect faults quickly
- Isolate faulty sections
- Protect equipment
- Maintain continuity of supply

---

# Need for Substation Protection

Power system faults can cause:

- Equipment damage
- Fire hazards
- Power interruption
- System instability

Protection systems minimize these effects.

# Types of Substation Faults

## 2. Earth Faults

Occurs when a conductor comes in contact with earth.

## 3. Transformer Faults

# 30. Protective Relays Used in Substations

Protective relays detect abnormal conditions and send trip commands.

---

# 1. Overcurrent Relay

Protects against excessive current.

Applications:

- Feeders
- Distribution systems

---

# 2. Differential Relay

Works on the principle of current difference.

\[
I_{diff}=I_1-I_2
\]

If:

\[
I_{diff}\neq0
\]

a fault is detected.

Applications:

- Transformers
- Generators
- Busbars

---

# 3. Distance Relay

Measures impedance between relay location and fault point.

\[
Z=\frac{V}{I}
\]

Applications:

- Transmission line protection

---

# 4. Buchholz Relay

Used for oil-filled transformers.

Detects:

- Gas formation
- Internal transformer faults

---

# 31. Busbar Protection

Busbars are critical points in substations.

A busbar fault can affect many connected circuits.

---

# Differential Busbar Protection

The relay compares:

- Incoming current
- Outgoing current

During normal operation:

\[
\sum I_{in}=\sum I_{out}
\]

During fault:

\[
\sum I_{in}\neq\sum I_{out}
\]

The relay trips circuit breakers.

Neutral point of electrical systems is connected to earth.

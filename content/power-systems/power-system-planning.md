# 1. Introduction to Power System Planning

A power system must continuously expand and improve to meet increasing electricity demand.

Population growth, industrial development, urbanization, and technological advancement increase the requirement for electrical energy.

Power system planning is the process of deciding:

- How much generation capacity is required
- Where new power plants should be installed
- How transmission networks should be expanded
- How distribution systems should be improved

The objective is to provide reliable and economical power supply for future demand.

---

# Definition

**Power system planning** is the process of forecasting future electrical demand and developing suitable generation, transmission, and distribution facilities to satisfy that demand reliably and economically.

---

# Need for Power System Planning

Power system planning is required because:

- Electricity demand continuously increases.
- Existing equipment has limited capacity.
- New industries require additional power.
- Renewable energy sources need integration.
- Reliability requirements are increasing.

# 11. Installed Capacity and Demand

The total installed generation capacity must be greater than maximum demand.

\[
Installed\ Capacity > Maximum\ Demand
\]

The difference provides reserve capacity.

---

# 12. Reserve Margin

Reserve margin is the additional generation capacity available above the expected peak demand.

---

# Formula

\[
Reserve\ Margin =
\frac{Available\ Capacity - Peak\ Demand}
{Peak\ Demand}
\times100
\]

---

# Importance of Reserve Margin

It provides protection against:

- Generator failure
- Unexpected demand increase
- Maintenance shutdowns

---

# Example

If:

Available capacity = 1200 MW

Peak demand = 1000 MW

\[
Reserve\ Margin=
\frac{1200-1000}{1000}\times100
\]

\[
=20\%
\]

# 17. Factors Considered in Transmission Planning

## 1. Power Flow Requirements

The network must be capable of carrying future power flows.

Studies include:

- Load flow analysis
- Contingency analysis

---

## 2. Voltage Stability

The transmission system must maintain acceptable voltage levels.

Poor planning may cause:

- Voltage drop
- Voltage collapse

---

## 3. Fault Level

New transmission additions increase short circuit levels.

The equipment rating must be selected accordingly.

---

## 4. System Reliability

The network should continue operation even after certain failures.

This is called:

**N-1 Security Criterion**

---

# N-1 Security Criterion

The system should operate normally even after failure of one major component.

Example:

```
Normal Condition

Generator ─── Line ─── Load


After One Line Failure


Generator ─── Alternate Line ─── Load

System Still Operating

```

# 21. Optimal Power System Planning

Optimal planning selects the best expansion strategy considering technical and economic factors.

---

# Planning Objective

The objective is:

\[
\text{Minimize Total Cost}
\]

while satisfying:

- Reliability constraints
- Security requirements
- Technical limits

---

# Total Planning Cost

Total cost includes:

\[
C_{total}=C_i+C_o+C_m+C_f
\]

where:

- \(C_i\) = Investment cost
- \(C_o\) = Operating cost
- \(C_m\) = Maintenance cost
- \(C_f\) = Fuel cost

---

# Optimization Techniques

Various mathematical methods are used:

---

# 1. Linear Programming

Used for optimization problems with linear relationships.

Applications:

- Generation planning
- Network expansion

---

# 2. Dynamic Programming

Used when decisions are made over multiple stages.

Applications:

- Expansion scheduling

---

# 3. Artificial Intelligence Methods

Modern planning uses:

- Genetic algorithms
- Neural networks
- Particle swarm optimization

---

# 22. Probabilistic Planning

# Probabilistic Planning Uses:

# Deterministic vs Probabilistic Planning

# Bus Bars, Circuit Breakers and Isolators

Power system components are essential for transmitting, controlling, and isolating electrical power safely within substations and electrical networks.

<img src="assets/animations/power-components.svg" alt="Bus Bars, Circuit Breakers and Isolators">

---

# Bus Bars

A **bus bar** is a thick conductor made of copper or aluminium that collects electrical power from one point and distributes it to multiple outgoing circuits.

Bus bars are commonly used in substations, switchgear panels, distribution boards, and power plants.

---

## Why Bus Bars are Used

- Carry very large currents
- Connect several incoming and outgoing feeders
- Reduce wiring complexity
- Improve system reliability
- Simplify maintenance and expansion

---

## Construction

A bus bar is usually made of:

- Copper
- Aluminium

It may have different shapes such as:

- Flat strip
- Round rod
- Hollow tube

The bus bar is mounted on insulators to prevent leakage current.

---

## Working Principle

Electrical power from generators or transformers enters the bus bar.

The bus bar then distributes the power to different feeders or circuits connected to it.

```
Generator/Transformer
          │
          ▼
      ─────────────
      │  Bus Bar  │
      ─────────────
      │    │    │
      ▼    ▼    ▼
 Feeder1 Feeder2 Feeder3
```

---

## Advantages

- Can carry high current
- Low power loss
- Easy fault isolation
- Easy to extend the system
- Simple maintenance

---

## Disadvantages

- High installation cost
- Requires proper insulation
- Short circuit currents can be very high

---

## Applications

- Electrical substations
- Switchgear panels
- Distribution boards
- Industrial power systems
- Power plants

---

# Circuit Breaker

A **circuit breaker** is a switching device that can make, carry, and safely interrupt current under both normal and fault conditions.

Unlike a fuse, it can be reset and used again after clearing a fault.

---

## Purpose

The circuit breaker protects electrical equipment from:

- Short circuits
- Overloads
- Earth faults
- Excessive current

---

## Basic Construction

A circuit breaker consists of:

- Fixed contact
- Moving contact
- Arc extinguishing chamber
- Operating mechanism
- Insulating enclosure

---

## Working Principle

### Normal Condition

- Contacts remain closed.
- Current flows normally.

### Fault Condition

- Relay detects the fault.
- Trip signal is sent.
- Contacts separate.
- Arc is produced.
- Arc is extinguished.
- Current flow stops.

```
Normal

Source ─────[ Closed Contacts ]──── Load

Fault

Source ─────[ Open Contacts ]      Load
                ✨ Arc ✨
```

---

## Types of Circuit Breakers

### Air Circuit Breaker (ACB)

- Uses air to extinguish the arc
- Used in low-voltage systems

---

### Oil Circuit Breaker (OCB)

- Uses insulating oil
- Good cooling properties
- Older technology

---

### Vacuum Circuit Breaker (VCB)

- Arc is extinguished inside a vacuum
- Very reliable
- Low maintenance
- Widely used in medium-voltage systems

---

### SF₆ Circuit Breaker

- Uses sulfur hexafluoride gas
- Excellent insulation
- High breaking capacity
- Used in high-voltage transmission

---

## Advantages

- Automatic protection
- Can be reused
- Fast operation
- High reliability
- Better safety than fuses

---

## Disadvantages

- More expensive than fuses
- Requires periodic maintenance
- Complex construction

---

## Applications

- Power stations
- Transmission systems
- Distribution substations
- Industrial installations
- Commercial buildings

---

# Isolator

An **isolator** is a mechanical switching device used to completely disconnect a part of the electrical system for maintenance.

It is also called a **disconnect switch**.

---

## Important Point

An isolator is **never operated while current is flowing**.

It is opened only after the circuit breaker has interrupted the current.

---

## Purpose

- Provide visible isolation
- Ensure maintenance safety
- Prevent accidental energization
- Disconnect faulty equipment

---

## Working Principle

### Step 1

Circuit breaker interrupts the current.

### Step 2

Current becomes zero.

### Step 3

Isolator opens the circuit.

Since no current flows, there is no arc.

```
Circuit Breaker OFF
          │
          ▼
      Isolator OPEN

Equipment is safely isolated.
```

---

## Types of Isolators

### Single Break Isolator

- One break per pole
- Simple construction

---

### Double Break Isolator

- Two breaks per pole
- Better voltage distribution

---

### Pantograph Isolator

- Used in Extra High Voltage (EHV) substations
- Requires less horizontal space

---

## Advantages

- High safety
- Simple operation
- Low maintenance
- Visible isolation
- Long service life

---

## Disadvantages

- Cannot interrupt load current
- Must always operate with a circuit breaker
- Not suitable for fault interruption

---

## Applications

- Electrical substations
- High-voltage transmission lines
- Transformer maintenance
- Generator isolation
- Switchyards

---

# Difference Between Circuit Breaker and Isolator

| Circuit Breaker | Isolator |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Interrupts load current | Cannot interrupt load current |
| Operates during faults | Operates only after breaker opens |
| Arc is produced | No arc is produced |
| Used for protection | Used for isolation |
| Automatic or manual | Mostly manual |

---

# Summary

- **Bus Bar** distributes electrical power to multiple circuits.
- **Circuit Breaker** protects the system by interrupting fault current.
- **Isolator** disconnects equipment safely for maintenance.
- Circuit breakers and isolators are often used together in substations to ensure both protection and safety.

# Capacitor Banks, Reactors, Surge Arresters and Insulators

Power systems require several supporting components to improve efficiency, maintain voltage levels, protect equipment, and ensure safe operation. Some of the most important components are capacitor banks, reactors, surge arresters, and insulators.

---

# Capacitor Banks

A **capacitor bank** is a group of capacitors connected together to store electrical energy and supply reactive power to the power system.

Capacitor banks are mainly used for **power factor improvement** and **voltage regulation**.

---

## Why are Capacitor Banks Needed?

Most industrial loads such as induction motors and transformers consume reactive power.

This causes:

- Low power factor
- Increased current
- Higher transmission losses
- Voltage drop

Capacitor banks supply reactive power locally, reducing the burden on the power system.

---

## Working Principle

Capacitors produce leading reactive power.

When connected across the load, they compensate for the lagging reactive power drawn by inductive loads.

```
Power Source
      │
      ▼
   Capacitor Bank
      │
      ▼
     Load
```

---

## Advantages

- Improves power factor
- Reduces line current
- Decreases power losses
- Improves voltage profile
- Increases system efficiency
- Reduces electricity charges in industries

---

## Disadvantages

- High initial cost
- Overcompensation may occur
- Sensitive to harmonics
- Requires periodic maintenance

---

## Applications

- Industrial plants
- Distribution substations
- Power factor correction panels
- Commercial buildings
- Transmission systems

---

# Reactors

A **reactor** is an inductor used in power systems to limit current or absorb reactive power.

Unlike capacitors, reactors consume reactive power.

---

## Purpose of Reactors

Reactors are used to:

- Limit fault current
- Reduce switching surges
- Control voltage
- Absorb excess reactive power
- Protect equipment

---

## Working Principle

A reactor offers inductive reactance to alternating current.

This limits excessive current during faults or switching operations.

---

## Types of Reactors

### Series Reactor

Connected in series with the line to limit fault current.

---

### Shunt Reactor

Connected in parallel to absorb excess reactive power and control overvoltage in long transmission lines.

---

## Advantages

- Limits fault current
- Protects electrical equipment
- Controls system voltage
- Improves system stability

---

## Disadvantages

- Voltage drop
- Additional power loss
- Higher installation cost

---

## Applications

- Transmission systems
- Substations
- Power plants
- Industrial electrical networks

---

# Surge Arresters

A **surge arrester** is a protective device used to protect electrical equipment from high-voltage surges caused by lightning or switching operations.

---

## Why are Surge Arresters Needed?

Lightning strikes and switching operations can produce very high voltages.

These surges may damage:

- Transformers
- Circuit breakers
- Generators
- Transmission equipment
- Insulation systems

A surge arrester safely diverts the surge current to earth.

---

## Working Principle

Under normal voltage:

- The arrester behaves like an insulator.

During a surge:

- It becomes conductive.
- The excess voltage is diverted safely to the ground.
- After the surge, it returns to its insulating state.

```
Lightning Surge
        │
        ▼
 Surge Arrester
        │
        ▼
     Earth
```

---

## Advantages

- Protects expensive equipment
- Fast operation
- Improves system reliability
- Long service life
- Low maintenance

---

## Applications

- Transmission lines
- Distribution systems
- Substations
- Power plants
- Industrial installations

---

# Insulators

An **insulator** is a material or device that supports electrical conductors while preventing unwanted current flow to the ground or supporting structures.

---

## Functions of Insulators

- Support conductors
- Prevent leakage current
- Provide electrical insulation
- Maintain safe conductor spacing

---

## Properties of a Good Insulator

A good insulator should have:

- High electrical resistance
- High mechanical strength
- Good weather resistance
- High dielectric strength
- Long service life

---

## Types of Insulators

### Pin Insulator

Used for low and medium-voltage overhead lines.

---

### Suspension Insulator

Used in high-voltage transmission lines.

---

### Strain Insulator

Used where the conductor experiences high mechanical tension, such as at dead ends and sharp bends.

---

### Shackle Insulator

Used in low-voltage distribution systems.

---

## Common Insulating Materials

- Porcelain
- Glass
- Polymer (Composite)

---

## Advantages

- Prevents leakage current
- Supports conductors safely
- High mechanical strength
- Long operational life
- Weather resistant

---

## Disadvantages

- Can crack due to mechanical stress
- May become contaminated by dust and pollution
- Requires periodic inspection

---

## Applications

- Transmission lines
- Distribution lines
- Electrical substations
- Railway electrification
- Outdoor switchyards

---

# Summary

| Component | Main Function |

- **Capacitor Bank** → Improves power factor.
